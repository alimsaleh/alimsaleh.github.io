Philosophy

Simple does not mean boring

People often confuse simplicity with less functionality or even few results. I quite believe the opposite and here is why. Think about the last time a kid complained to you about not being able to use their iPad ‚Äì doesn‚Äôt really happen that often. There is so much functionality behind the simple screen of an iPad ‚Äì and it is very complicated as well ‚Äì yet a 4 years old kid is able to achieve their goals with minimal to no effort at all. In a few words, I would define simplicity as the ability to design a product that feels like second nature to users when they interact with it.

Focus on intent not desire

One of my favorite quotes about innovation and design attributed to Henry Ford goes like this ‚ÄúIf I had asked people what they wanted, they would have said faster horses.‚Äù This doesn‚Äôt mean that people always don‚Äôt know what they want rather it means that it is our job as designers and innovators to bring the right conversation to the table. In other words, to get the right answers you need to ask the rights questions first.

Less is sometimes more

The law of diminishing returns ‚Äì does it sound familiar? It basically states that ‚Äúat some point, adding an additional factor of production results in smaller increases in output‚Äù. Sometimes, product teams get carried away with the technical abilities or visual effects of their products to the point where users are overwhelmed and experience less satisfaction with the product. I believe this is one of the most critical points that everyone in a product team (mangers, designers, architects, developers etc.) need to be conscious and aware of to avoid any self-inflected damage to a product.

Users are partners before they are customers

Users should be involved and engaged in all phases of product development and their feedback is too valuable to be overlooked at any phase in the process. The more distance development teams have between them and their users the more likely the product will be poorly received once it is released. In my opinion, development teams should aim to amaze their users not necessarily surprise them ‚Äì there are always bad surprises üòä.

Take a step back to move forward

Never lose sight of the big picture. I think people sometimes dwell on attractive new ideas or tiny details which may or may not be relevant to the original goals they set to achieve. Yes, teams need to be agile and flexible but that doesn‚Äôt mean the price should be to lose perspective and forget about the broader objective. Having checkpoints and mission reminders along the way will always keep the teams focused, more productive, and energized throughout the development journey.

Companies aim to have their users spend more time on their products/services presumably to increase profits. However, users tend to pay a hefty price for such misuse and turn into tech addicts. According to The Telegraph newspaper ‚Äúthose aged 16 to 24 average 34.3 hours a week on the internet‚Äù which translates to roughly 5 hours a day. Additionally, users have the options to set usage limits and apply parental controls on their family members‚Äô devices. In this assessment, I want to reveal some insights on the goals users want to achieve from using Apple‚Äôs Screen Time Control and what challenges they might run into along the way.
